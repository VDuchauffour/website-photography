My name is **Karel Novak** and I photograph buildings that most people walk past without a second glance.

Based in Prague, I spend my weekends hunting for brutalist towers, crumbling modernist housing blocks, and the strange beauty hiding in postwar concrete. What started as idle curiosity during architecture school became an obsession — the way raw concrete catches low winter light, the repetition of balconies on a panel building, the alien geometry of a 1970s civic centre.

I shoot exclusively on weekends and holidays. This is not my profession — I work as a structural engineer during the week. The photography is the counterbalance. Where my day job demands precision and safety margins, the camera lets me see these same structures as pure form, texture, and shadow.

My work focuses on **postwar architecture across Central and Eastern Europe** — the housing estates of Prague, the concrete churches of Switzerland, the brutalist universities of England, the Soviet-era monuments scattered across the Balkans. I'm drawn to buildings that divide opinion: structures most consider ugly that I find deeply compelling.

Everything is shot handheld with natural light. No drones, no HDR, no heavy post-processing. I want the images to feel the way these buildings feel when you stand in front of them — imposing, textural, unapologetic.
