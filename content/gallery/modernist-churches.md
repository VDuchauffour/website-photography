Postwar concrete churches represent brutalism at its most expressive. Freed from the rectangular demands of housing or offices, architects used raw concrete to create genuinely sculptural sacred spaces. Light enters through unexpected angles. Walls fold and cantilever. The material itself becomes spiritual.

Fritz Wotruba's church in Vienna — a pile of asymmetric concrete blocks — is the movement's masterpiece. But equally compelling are the lesser-known pilgrimage churches and parish buildings scattered across Germany, France, and Switzerland.
